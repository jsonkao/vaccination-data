Asians Lead Vaccination Rate in More Than 20 States: Covid-19 Tracker

Racial gaps remain as states start offering vaccines to the general population
Updated: April 7, 2021, 6:00 AM EDT

Vaccine Tracker

  • Global
  • U.S.
  • U.S. Vaccine Demographics
  • FAQ
  • Covid-19 Tracker

The White vaccination rate has consistently outpaced every other race, but almost half of the states Bloomberg tracks are now doing even better with their Asian populations.

More states have vaccinated at least 35% of Asian people than have reached that same milestone for their White populations. New Mexico has vaccinated more than half of its Asian population, and Mississippi almost 43% of Asian people in the state.

Asian Americans, on the whole, have seen the lowest death rates from Covid among racial groups since the pandemic began, according to the APM Research Lab. Though, the last month has been the deadliest for the group, APM found.

As states open up vaccine eligibility to more people, Black and Hispanic inoculation rates continue to lag. Only two states, New Mexico and Oregon, have vaccinated 25% of their Black populations, while three dozen states have done just as well with White people, and two dozen as well with Asians.

Since last week, 35 states and New York City have narrowed the gap between the White and Black share of people vaccinated, including half a dozen that narrowed it by more than one percentage point. There were a few states—including Alaska and Idaho—where the gap has been widening.

As of this week, our tracker has data from 43 states, D.C. and two cities that cover 94.2% of people and 92.8% of doses administered.

The reasons for any improvements are varied. Some cities, states and hospitals have started targeting minority populations, while shots are reaching a broader and more diverse slice of the population as the vaccination drive picks up speed.

Some of the remaining disparities are still a result of who had earliest approval to get shots. The elderly are more likely to be White, while the Hispanic population skews young and is less likely to work in hospitals and nursing care, groups targeted in the earliest phases of vaccine distribution.

Other disparities are a result of lower uptake rates among certain groups or less access to shots. Some health-care settings have reported that it’s taking longer to build up trust with communities of color, particularly Black people, who are wary of a medical establishment that for centuries has ignored and mistreated them. Anti-vaxx misinformation campaigns are targeting the already hesitant, including women and Black people. In addition, those with fewer resources may not be able to navigate the notoriously buggy and overloaded online sign-up systems. Meanwhile, others with connections, time, and money can snap up open slots.

As vaccines become available to the broader population, the equity of the campaign will become clearer, and Bloomberg’s tracker is designed to monitor the progress as it evolves.

Data on vaccinations among Native American and Native Alaskan groups are excluded because the Indian Health Service, which is coordinating much of the rollout, hasn’t reported state-by-state numbers. Alaska is the only state not relying on the IHS, and has so far vaccinated 42% of this group.

Federal and state officials have said they’ve made it a priority to get those most at risk vaccinated quickly. Covid infection rates in certain minority groups have consistently outpaced those in White communities; death rates for some Black counties were triple those of comparable White ones. As data start to emerge that richer, Whiter areas are getting more than their fair share of inoculations, officials have announced a series of initiatives, like mass vaccination sites and partnerships with pharmacies in underserved areas, to better target vulnerable minority communities.

As of this week, North Carolina scores best on data quality for the sixth week in a row, reporting race and ethnicity data for nearly 100% of people vaccinated in the state. On the other end of the spectrum is Hawaii, with closer to 80% of data coverage for race—the state doesn’t report ethnicity. Alabama and Michigan improved their data quality the most from last week.

The reasons for such wide variation in data quality remain unclear. Providers are supposed to collect demographic information. They might not always ask for or record it correctly. Some people prefer not to share their background or might not understand why it’s important to do so, according to Nirav Shah, the director of the Maine Center for Disease Control and Prevention, who spoke during a press briefing in February. Mississippi, for its part, has identified issues with sending electronic health records into state registry.

Early indications of inequity should be a clarion call for better, more complete data on race and ethnicity. Without it, we won’t be able to fully assess how well—or how poorly—the U.S. is serving all of its residents.

By: Rachael Dottle, Akayla Gardner, Jeff Green, Angelica LaVito, Marie Patino, Andre Tartar, Alexander McIntyre, Aaron Kessler and Carolina Gonzalez
Editors: Rebecca Greenfield and Yue Qiu
With the assistance of: Julie Alnwick Mchale
Methodology

The weekly demographic tracker looks at 43 states, the District of Columbia, plus two cities—New York City and Philadelphia—where there’s available race or ethnicity data about who’s getting vaccinated, collectively accounting for 92.8% of all U.S. Covid-19 doses administered. (The tracker will include more states as they disclose relevant information.)

We use the most recent Census 5-year American Community Survey to calculate each race and ethnicity’s share of a city or state’s population. This data include all ages, even though vaccines have not yet been approved for children and some teens. North Dakota and Vermont only provide vaccination data as a share of the underlying population, so their numbers are directly reported in the tracker.

Available demographic data for vaccines are not of equal quality. To assess the differences, Bloomberg has assigned a data quality score out of 100 to each state or city. The following dimensions are taken into account in assigning the scores:

  • Does a jurisdiction provide racial or ethnic data that can be compared to Census data?
  • Does a jurisdiction report race and ethnicity separately or not? The former is preferred as it allows for a more expansive definition of available racial categories (see explanation below).
  • What share of people receiving at least one vaccine dose have a race assigned to them?
  • What share of people receiving at least one dose have an ethnicity assigned to them?

Those that report no race or ethnicity data will get a score of zero. Three states report demographic data in terms of doses administered or some other form that can’t be compared to their overall population, and get a score of ten. For the remaining states, ratings depend on how much race and ethnicity data are missing.

Reporting race and ethnicity separately, which more than half of states do, gives the most representative view. In those places, people who identify as both Black and Hispanic will be counted under both race and ethnicity. In states that combine race and ethnicity, they will only be counted in the “Hispanic of all races” category, giving a narrower view of Black vaccination rates. We’ve accounted for this, and any other variations in how states report race and ethnicity, when deciding which specific Census population data to compare in each case.

Other additional data issues to consider: Cities and states are vaccinating non-residents, though available data indicate they account for less than 10% of those vaccinated. DC, Idaho, New York City, Philadelphia, South Carolina, Virginia and Wisconsin separate out the data for residents receiving shots. Some states provide data on people who identify as Native Americans, Native Hawaiians or Pacific Islanders, other races or as multi-racial, but because of the variation in how they’re reported by states, we are excluding them from the tracker. The vaccinated share of each group is likely an underestimate due to White, Black, Asian and Hispanic people who are instead counted under other races, multi-racial, or for whom race or ethnicity data is missing. Time periods for different states’ demographic data may differ based on reporting frequency and lags.
